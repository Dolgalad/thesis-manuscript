\section{Integer Linear Programing}
\label{sec:co_intro}

Contributions of this thesis focus on the problem of solving Integer Linear programming problems (ILPs) that are commonly encountered in the field of combinatorial optimization. We particularly focus on \emph{binary integer linear programs} which is the class of problems for which all decision variables are binary and is a class of problems listed in \cite{bohlinger_complexity_1972} as one of Karp's 21 NP-Complete problems. In our work we do not delve deeply into the approaches used to solve ILPs, but certain algorithms do play a central role in the frameworks presented. In this section we will give an overview of ILPs, their definition as an optimization problem, methods for solving them either exact or heuristic, areas that could potentialy be subject to improvement using learning techniques and an introduction to the particular classes of problems we will be dealing with.

\subsection{A brief historical overview}
Combinatorial optimization has a long history, with problems arising naturally in tasks such as finding efficient paths for foraging or planning routes. A systematic mathematical study of CO emerged in the 20th century, leading to a unified field encompassing graph theory, operations research, and integer programming \cite{schrijver_history_2005}. 

\input{resources/tikz/chapter_1/co/co_event_timeline}

The terms \emph{combinatorial optimization} and \emph{discrete optimization} are often used interchangeably. The former emphasizes that the underlying search space is combinatorial, e.g.\ involving subsets of vertices, edges, or paths, while the latter stresses the distinction from continuous optimization. Indeed, many discrete optimization methods borrow tools from continuous optimization, such as linear programming relaxations and polyhedral methods.

Formally, combinatorial optimization problems are typically expressed as the minimization of a cost function defined over binary or integer decision variables. The problems of interest in this work can be written as integer linear programs with binary variables. While the methods presented are broadly applicable to discrete optimization, in specific applications we exploit the combinatorial structure of the solution space to design more effective solvers.

\subsection{Problem Definition}
The \emph{canonical form} of an ILP program is given as
\begin{align}
    \label{equ:ilp_canonical_form}
    \begin{array}{ll}
        \max & c^T x \\
        \text{s.t.}              & A x \leq b \\
                                 & x \in \mathcal{X}
    \end{array}
\end{align}
where $x\in \mathcal{X} \subseteq \Z^n$ are the \emph{decision variables} of the problem, $c\in\R^n$ a vector of costs and $A,b\in\R^{m\times n} \times \R^m$ define the constraints of the problem. Binary integer linear programs (BILP), on which we will be focusing on in the following work, are problems for which $\mathcal{X} = \{0,1\}^n$. An assignment $x$ is called \emph{feasible} if it belongs to the set $\mathcal{F} := \{ x | A x \leq b \}$ (hereafter referred to as the feasible set) of assignments that do not violate the constraints of the problem.

An LP is said to be \emph{compact} (\cite{yannakakis_expressing_1988,carr_compact_2000} if the number of constraints $m$ used to defined the feasible set $\mathcal{F}$ is polynomial in the size of the input. The work presented in this thesis makes extensive use of methods for representing problems as graphs, as such the number of variables and constraints of the problems must be of reasonable size. In non-compact LPs the number of constraints needed to defined the feasible set may be so large that it cannot be reasonably be represented in memory. Mainly we will focus our work on problems for which a compact formulation exists.


\subsection{Solving ILPs}
Solving ILPs problems efficiently has been the subject of extensive work since the formalization of the field of research. These types of problems may often be tackled by multiple methods, either exploiting the specifics of the problem at hand or by using a more general approach. Consider shortest path problems for example; the structure of the solutions of these problems can be easily represented as a collection of constraints representing the conservation of flow at each node, leading to the formalization of the problem as an ILP. The state of the art methods for solving ILPs can be used to solve such problems. However in practice it is better to leverage the specifics of the problem to define a solving approach that makes direct use of the solution structure to solve the problem, in this case the Dijkstra algorithm is a much more efficient method of solving the problem than using a generic ILP solver. Leveraging knowledge of the structure of the solution space can lead to large improvements of the solver capabilities when compared to generic approaches and can also lead to very efficient approximation schemes. As pointed out these approaches for solving these problems rely on knowledge of the structure of the problem and can thus not be used for general CO problems. In the works presented in further sections we have chosen to consider the problem of solving ILPs in their general framework, and thus we focus on general approaches for solving ILPs relying on domain specific knowledge mostly as a corrective measure to ensure that the solutions provided by our frameworks do not violate the constraints of the problem.

In the following subsection we will give a brief review of solving strategies that can be applied to ILPs, both exact and heuristic.

\paragraph{Cutting planes}  
The introduction of cutting planes in 1958 by R. E. Gomory, marked a turning point in the study of linear programs and lead to an increased interest in particularities linked to dealing with integer variables in these types of problems. Cutting planes strengthen the continuous relaxation by iteratively adding linear inequalities (cuts) that remove infeasible fractional solutions while preserving all integer feasible solutions. Since their introduction, cutting-plane methods have since been refined and are widely used in practice, often in combination with B\&B (the Branch-and-Cut framework). Consider an LP problem with a set of binary decision variables $x \in \Z^n$ and a feasible set defined by a set of linear inequalities $\mathcal{F} = \{ x | A x \leq b \}$. $\mathcal{F}$ is a polyhedron who's vertices are not necessarily integer points and thus not solutions to the ILP. If $\mathcal{F}$'s vertices were integer points the problems would be easily solvable by relying on LP solver literature. In practice this is not the case. The set of integer points that are within the feasible set is $\mathcal{F} \cap \Z^n$. It would thus be desirable to solve the optimization problem over the set $\text{Conv}(\mathcal{F}\cap \Z^n)$, the convex hull of all integer feasible solutions. Cutting plane methods aim to successively "cut" the feasible space eliminating non-integer feasible solutions so that the feasible set converges to the convex hull of integer feasible solutions. 

\input{resources/tikz/chapter_1/co/cutting_plane}

In Branch-and-Bound, the search space is recursively partitioned by branching on variables. Branch-and-Cut extends this framework by strengthening the LP relaxation at each node using cutting planes: valid inequalities that remove fractional LP solutions without excluding any integer-feasible points. At a node, the LP relaxation is solved and a separation routine attempts to identify violated cuts. These cuts are added to tighten the relaxation, which is then re-optimized. If no further violated cuts can be found and the LP solution is still fractional, the algorithm proceeds to branch, thereby creating subproblems exactly as in Branch-and-Bound. If the LP relaxation becomes integer-feasible, the node can be pruned or its solution used as an incumbent. \subsubsection{Heuristics}
Heuristics play a crucial role in solving ILPs. It is important to make the distinction with approximation schemes for which approximation bounds are known. Heuristics are methods for creating feasible solutions to the problem, that is finding a point that lies withing the feasible region and is integer. They can be used within exact solvers to generate candidate solutions, approximate lower bounds at a node of a B\&B tree. In this section we will give a brief presentation of a selection of heuristics.

Because exact approaches scale poorly, approximate methods are indispensable in practice.  

\paragraph{Diving/Depth-First Search/Breadth-First Search} These are among the simplest node-selection strategies used to traverse a Branch-and-Bound tree.
Diving refers to following a single branch of the tree downward as far as possible—typically by repeatedly selecting a child node with the smallest estimated bound—until reaching a leaf node or an infeasible relaxation. It does not allow backtracking within the heuristic; once the dive ends, control returns to the general B\&B search strategy.

By contrast, Depth-First Search (DFS) and Breadth-First Search (BFS) are full-fledged search strategies that do support backtracking. DFS prioritizes exploring the deepest unexplored node first, systematically backtracking when a subtree is exhausted. BFS explores the tree level by level, expanding all nodes at depth $d$ before moving to depth $d+1$. Both DFS and BFS ensure completeness of the search, unlike diving, which is a heuristic used only to obtain feasible solutions quickly or guide the search in promising directions.

\paragraph{Rounding/Feasibility Pump} Another class of primal heuristics attempts to construct an integer solution that is close to the fractional LP relaxation point. Simple rounding strategies directly round fractional variables to nearby integer values and may apply repair procedures to restore feasibility. In contrast, the Feasibility Pump adopts a more elaborate alternating scheme: it iteratively projects the current fractional LP solution $\bar{x}$ onto the integer domain , typically by solving a rounding or proximity optimization problem (see Equation~\ref{eq:fp_proximity_obj} and then projects the resulting integer point back into the LP relaxation. This alternating process continues until an integer-feasible solution is found or a stopping criterion is met.

\begin{align}
    x^{I} \in 
    \arg\min_{x} \quad 
        & \sum_{i \in I} \left| x_i - \bar{x}_i \right|  \label{eq:fp_proximity_obj} \\
    \text{s.t.} \quad
        & A x \le b, \label{eq:fp_constraints} \\
        & \ell \le x \le u, \label{eq:fp_bounds} \\
        & x \in \mathbb{Z}^n 
\end{align}

\paragraph{Neighborhood-Based Improvement Heuristics (RINS and VNS).}
Neighborhood-based improvement heuristics attempt to refine a current incumbent integer solution by exploring a restricted subset of the feasible region. Two representative methods are \emph{Relaxation Induced Neighborhood Search} (RINS) and \emph{Variable Neighborhood Search} (VNS).

Relaxation Induced Neighborhood Search (RINS) \cite{danna_exploring_2005} constructs its neighborhood by exploiting the structural relationship between the incumbent solution $x^{\text{inc}}$ and the optimal fractional LP relaxation $\bar{x}$. Let $I$ be the set of integer indices, and define
\[
    F = \{\, i \in I : x^{\text{inc}}_i = \lfloor \bar{x}_i \rceil \,\}.
\]
RINS then fixes the variables in $F$ and solves the reduced MILP
\[
    \min\{\, c^\top x : Ax \le b,\; x_i = x^{\text{inc}}_i \ (i \in F),\; x_j \in \mathbb{Z} \ (j \in I \setminus F)\,\},
\]
which defines a neighborhood containing all feasible solutions consistent with the common components of $x^{\text{inc}}$ and $\bar{x}$.

VNS, in contrast, defines a sequence of expanding combinatorial neighborhoods around the incumbent. For binary or integer variables, a typical choice is the $k$-flip neighborhood
\[
    \mathcal{N}_k(x^{\text{inc}}) = \{\, x \in X : \Delta(x, x^{\text{inc}}) = k \,\},
\]
where $\Delta(\cdot,\cdot)$ denotes the Hamming distance. A generic VNS iteration consists of:
(i) a \emph{shaking} step that selects a point $x' \in \mathcal{N}_k(x^{\text{inc}})$,
(ii) a local improvement phase applied to $x'$, and
(iii) an update rule that either accepts the improved solution (and resets $k=1$) or increases the neighborhood size $k \leftarrow k+1$.
This systematic enlargement of neighborhoods allows VNS to escape local minima while intensifying search around promising regions.

Both RINS and VNS follow the large-neighborhood search philosophy: they construct a restricted subproblem around the incumbent, attempt to improve it, and update the incumbent whenever a better solution is found.

\subsection{Variable-Constraint Bipartite Representation}
\label{sec:vcg_definition}

Representing an ILP as a graph will be central to several of the methods studied in this thesis. A graph representation of mixed-integer linear optimization problems was notably used in \cite{gasse_exact_2019} through the \emph{variable-constraint bipartite graph}.

\begin{definition}[Variable-constraint bipartite representation of an ILP]
Given an ILP instance $(P)$ in canonical form~\eqref{equ:ilp_canonical_form}, its \emph{variable-constraint bipartite representation} is the bipartite graph $\VCGFULL$, where the set $\VCGV$ contains one node for each decision variable and the set $\VCGC$ contains one node for each constraint, i.e., each row $A_i x \leq b_i$. The edge set $\VCGE \subseteq \VCGV \times \VCGC$ is defined by
\[
(i,j)\in \VCGE
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
A_{ji} \neq 0,
\]
that is, a variable node is connected to a constraint node whenever the corresponding variable appears with nonzero coefficient in that constraint.
\end{definition}

\input{resources/tikz/chapter_1/co/vcg_example}

The graph $\VCGFULL$ can be enriched with node and edge features. Node features may encode quantities associated with variables or constraints, such as objective coefficients, right-hand-side values, slack values, fractional values, dual information, or basis status. Edge features may similarly be used to encode the coefficients $A_{ij}$.

This representation offers several advantages. First, it provides a sparse and permutation-invariant encoding of the optimization problem structure, making it well suited to graph-based learning architectures. Second, it allows instances of different sizes to be processed within a common framework, and batched together as disconnected components of a larger graph. Finally, this representation is closely related to the factor graph formalism introduced later in Section~\ref{sec:mrf_intro}, which will be central to our Markov random field models.


\subsection{Representative problems}

\subsubsection{Maximum Independent Set (MIS)}  
The Maximum Independent Set problem is a classical NP-hard problem: given an undirected graph $\GFULL$, find the largest subset $S \subseteq \GV$ such that no two vertices in $S$ are adjacent. MIS is equivalent to finding the maximum clique in the complement graph, with applications in network analysis, scheduling, and computational biology. By introducing for each vertex a binary decision variable $x_i, i\in \GV$ the ILP formulation of the problem is
\begin{align}
    \label{equ:mis_formuation_edges}
    (P_\text{MIS}) \Leftrightarrow \left\{
    \begin{array}{lll}
        \max        & \mathds{1}^T x   & \\
        \text{s.t.} & x_i + x_j \leq 1 & \forall (i,j) \in \GE \\
                    & x \in \{0,1\}^n
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}

Notice that each constraint $x_i + x_j \leq 1$ ensures that no two adjacent vertices can belong to the independent set $S$ at the same time. The set $\{x | x_i + x_j \leq 1, \forall (i,j) \in E\}$ contains all independent sets. Although correct this formulation is not very strong as the constraints do not tightly surround the set of feasible solutions to the problem. The formulation can be strengthened by noticing that for all cliques $c \subseteq V$ there can be only one vertex belonging to $S$. By denoting $\mathcal{C}$ the set of cliques in $G$ we obtain the following formulation
\begin{align}
    \label{equ:mis_formuation_cliques}
    (P_\text{MIS}) \Leftrightarrow \left\{
    \begin{array}{lll}
        \max        & \mathds{1}^T x   & \\
        \text{s.t.} & \sum_{i\in c} x_i \leq 1 & \forall c \in \mathcal{C} \\
                    & x \in \{0,1\}^n
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
The number of cliques in an undirected graph can however be exponential in $n$ meaning that it may not be feasible to use this formulation. The set $\mathcal{C}$ can further be restricted to the set of \emph{maximal} cliques, i.e. cliques that are not strictly contained in another clique of the graph. Enumerating all maximal cliques is a difficult task in itself, however clique contraints can be added as needed to strengthen the formulation during solving. 

\subsubsection{Set Covering Problem (SC)}  
The Set Covering problem is defined as follows: given a set of items $\ITEMS = \{\ITEM_i\}_{1\leq i\leq m}$ and a collection of sets $\SETS$ the objective is to find the smallest subset of $\SETS = \{\SET_i\}_{1\leq i\leq n}$ such that each item in $\ITEMS$ belongs to at least one set $\SET_i$. For each set $\SET\in\SETS$ introduce a binary decision variable $x_{\SET}$ and denoting by $\SETI$ the sets that contain the $i$-th item, then the corresponding ILP is :
\begin{align}
    \label{equ:sc_formuation_cliques}
    (P_\text{SC}) \Leftrightarrow \left\{
    \begin{array}{lll}
        \min        & \mathds{1}^T x   & \\
        \text{s.t.} & \sum_{\SET\in\SETI} x_{\SET} \geq 1 & \forall 1 \leq i \leq m \\
                    & x \in \{0,1\}^n
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{align}
