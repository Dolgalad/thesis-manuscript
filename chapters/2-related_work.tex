\chapter{Related Work}\label{chap:chapter_2_related_work}
\chaptermark{Related Work}

Sections~\ref{sec:co_intro},~\ref{sec:ml_intro}, and~\ref{sec:mrf_intro} introduced the three ingredients used throughout this thesis : binary integer linear programming (BILP) as the main representation of the optimization problems considered here, neural models as trainable predictors on variable--constraint graphs, and MRFs as structured probabilistic models over coupled binary decision variables. This chapter positions the thesis with respect to the literature that combines machine learning with integer programming, structured prediction, and graphical-model inference.

The central question studied here is not whether machine learning can improve combinatorial optimization in general, but how learned models can exploit the dependency structure induced by BILP constraints. We therefore distinguish two levels of related work. The first consists of methods designed specifically for ILP, MILP, or BILP solving, such as learned branching, cutting, primal heuristics, neural diving, and solution prediction. The second consists of broader CO and neural combinatorial optimization methods, such as graph-based construction, diffusion, flow, and local-improvement approaches, which are relevant as modeling principles but are not usually formulated for generic BILP instances.

This chapter is organized around modeling choices rather than around a catalogue of individual papers. We distinguish methods according to whether they guide an external solver or directly predict solution variables, whether dependencies between variables are represented explicitly or only implicitly, whether the solution is produced in one pass or through iterative refinement, and whether feasibility is enforced by the model, by a decoder, by repair, or by a downstream solver. This perspective highlights the position of the present thesis: we use ILP-derived graphical structure to define explicit dependencies between binary decision variables, learn or instantiate MRF parameters from instance data, and use differentiable approximate inference to produce structured information that guides, but does not replace, subsequent decoding.

\section{Learning to Guide ILP Solvers}
\label{sec:rw_solver_guidance}

A large part of the literature on machine learning for ILPs studies how learned models can improve the behavior of exact or heuristic solvers. This line of work is important for the thesis because it shows that solver performance depends strongly on decisions that can be learned from data. However, its goal is different from ours: the learned model usually guides a solver state, whereas Chapters~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs} and~\ref{chapter:4_denoising_and_mrf_inference} learn structured predictions over the solution variables themselves.

\subsection{Learning solver-control decisions}
\label{subsec:rw_solver_control}

B\&B and B\&C solvers rely on many heuristic decisions, including branching-variable selection, node selection, cut generation, cut selection, restart policies, primal heuristics, and decomposition decisions. Learning-augmented solvers replace or augment some of these decisions with data-driven policies, while preserving the surrounding exact optimization framework. General surveys of this area include \cite{bengio_machine_2018,cappart_combinatorial_2023,scavuzzo_machine_2024}.

Branching has received particular attention. Early work framed branching as a supervised learning problem in which a model imitates strong branching scores from engineered solver features \cite{khalil_learning_2016}. Gasse et al. introduced a now-standard variable--constraint bipartite graph representation and used a graph neural network to imitate strong branching decisions \cite{gasse_exact_2019}. Subsequent work studied reinforcement learning and hybrid imitation/reinforcement formulations, where the goal is not only to mimic a local branching score but also to reduce global quantities such as tree size or solution time \cite{he_learning_2014,nair_solving_2020,ding_accelerating_2020}. More recent methods improve generalization, interpretability, or action modeling, for example through lookback information, pointer-based policies, symbolic branching rules, or multi-action branching strategies \cite{gupta_lookback_2022,wang_learning_2024,kuang_towards_2024,luttmann_multi-action_2025}.

Other learned solver-control methods operate at a broader scale. Learned node utilities, pruning rules, restart decisions, and cut-selection policies provide semi-global or global guidance for the search process \cite{he_learning_2014,lodi_learning_2017,tang_reinforcement_2020,balcan_learning_2024}. Related work learns when to invoke decomposition or other expensive solver operations based on instance or search-state features \cite{salvagnin_learning_2017}. These methods all share the same broad principle: the learned component estimates the usefulness of a solver action, while the solver remains responsible for enforcing feasibility, proving bounds, and returning certificates when possible.

\subsection{Primal heuristics, diving, and repair}
\label{subsec:rw_primal_heuristics}

A second solver-centered direction learns primal construction or repair heuristics. Classical methods such as the feasibility pump, diving, RINS, local branching, and variable-neighborhood search use rounding, fixing, local repair, or restricted subproblems to obtain feasible incumbent solutions \cite{fischetti_feasibility_2005,battiti_learning_2017}. Learning can enter these procedures by predicting promising variable fixings, warm starts, repair moves, or neighborhoods. Neural diving and related approaches use learned scores to construct partial assignments that are then completed, repaired, or solved by a conventional optimizer \cite{nair_solving_2020}.

These methods are closer to the present thesis than pure branching policies because they produce information about the solution variables. Nevertheless, the role of the learned model is still usually external to the structured probabilistic model over assignments. Feasibility is often recovered by a hand-designed propagation rule, a repair heuristic, or a downstream MILP solver. In contrast, the MRF predictors developed in Chapter~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs} use approximate graphical-model inference to propagate information between coupled decision variables before decoding. These inference outputs guide the decoder, but they do not by themselves guarantee feasibility.

\subsection{Limitations for structured solution prediction}
\label{subsec:rw_solver_guidance_limitations}

Learning-augmented branch-and-bound is highly relevant when the goal is to accelerate a complete solver, but it does not by itself answer the structured prediction problem studied here. A branching policy chooses the next solver action; it does not define a distribution over complete BILP assignments. A cut-selection policy chooses which inequalities to add; it does not produce structured beliefs over variables. Even learned primal heuristics often delegate constraint satisfaction to a repair or solver step. The present thesis instead asks whether learned predictions can be made more constraint-aware by introducing an explicit graphical-model layer between neural scoring and decoding. The resulting model does not directly output a feasible solution in one step; rather, it produces structured quantities, such as factor parameters or approximate marginals, that can guide subsequent decoding.

\section{Neural Solution Prediction for ILPs}
\label{sec:rw_solution_prediction}

A complementary body of work learns prediction signals that are used more directly during solution construction. For the purposes of this thesis, the most relevant case is prediction over the binary decision variables of a BILP: variable scores, partial assignments, candidate fixings, or refinement updates. Broader neural CO methods, such as autoregressive routing models or graph-specific construction policies, are also relevant because they illustrate alternative ways of producing structured discrete assignments, but they are not usually designed as generic BILP predictors. The relevant distinction is therefore not whether a model outputs a feasible solution directly. In most cases, including the methods developed in this thesis, learned predictions must still be combined with decoding, propagation, repair, or solver-side correction. The key question is how the learned information is used by that subsequent construction process: some methods provide mostly independent variable scores, while others use sequential construction, problem-specific architectures, solver-in-the-loop correction, learned local improvement, or structured inference to guide the decoder.

\subsection{Factorized variable prediction on graph representations}
\label{subsec:rw_factorized_prediction}

In ILP and MILP settings, a common approach is to represent the optimization instance by its variable--constraint graph and to use a GNN or attention model to output one score per decision variable. The variable--constraint graph of Gasse et al.~\cite{gasse_exact_2019}, originally introduced for learning branching policies, provides a generic representation of MILP structure and has also been used for variable-level prediction, neural diving, and solution construction \cite{nair_solving_2020,ding_accelerating_2020}. In the BILP setting, such scores are naturally interpreted as logits or probabilities for binary decision variables.

Related graph-CO methods use similar neural representations for problems such as maximum independent set, vertex cover, and max-cut, where message-passing networks can learn node scores or greedy construction policies from supervised or reinforcement signals \cite{dai_learning_2017,li_combinatorial_2018}. Attention-based or transformer-style models provide another way to score or select solution components, especially when the output has an ordered or set-like structure \cite{vinyals_pointer_2015,kool_attention_2018}. These works are relevant here as neural construction methods, but their problem-specific decoders should be distinguished from generic BILP decoding.

The advantage of factorized BILP prediction is scalability: the model outputs one score per variable and the decoder can be simple. The limitation is that the probabilistic output often treats variables as conditionally independent given the neural embedding. The GNN may encode some constraint information into each variable representation, but the output distribution itself does not explicitly represent local joint configurations, factor marginals, or constraint-level compatibility. This is the limitation addressed in Chapter~3, where the factorized Bernoulli output is replaced by an MRF-structured distribution over BILP variables. The resulting marginals still require a decoder to obtain a feasible assignment.

\subsection{Sequential and problem-specific construction}
\label{subsec:rw_sequential_problem_specific}

A second family of methods builds solutions through a sequence of decisions. Many of these methods are problem-specific rather than formulated for generic ILPs. Reinforcement-learning methods combined with graph embeddings have been used to learn greedy construction policies for graph optimization problems such as maximum independent set, minimum vertex cover, and related tasks \cite{dai_learning_2017,li_combinatorial_2018}. In routing and sequencing problems, pointer networks and attention-based decoders construct tours or schedules autoregressively by selecting one element at a time \cite{vinyals_pointer_2015,kool_attention_2018}. For set covering, set partitioning, and related problems, bipartite or hypergraph encodings are natural because the problem structure is defined by item--set or variable--constraint incidence relations \cite{gasse_exact_2019}.

These methods exploit strong inductive biases matched to a particular combinatorial family. This specialization can lead to strong empirical performance, but it limits direct transfer to arbitrary BILP formulations. Moreover, feasibility is usually maintained through a problem-specific construction rule or repair step rather than through a generic probabilistic model of variable dependencies. This is not fundamentally different from the present thesis in the sense that our method also relies on a decoder; the difference is that our decoder is guided by marginals obtained from an explicit factor graph derived from the BILP constraints. Empirical studies of neural heuristics for routing also show that learned construction policies may struggle to generalize to larger or structurally different instances, emphasizing the importance of understanding the inductive bias imposed by the model and decoder \cite{joshi_learning_2021}.

\subsection{Feasibility, decoding, and solver-in-the-loop correction}
\label{subsec:rw_decoding_and_correction}

A recurring issue in neural solution prediction is that accurate local scores do not necessarily imply a feasible or high-quality global assignment. Existing approaches handle this issue in several ways. Some methods use handcrafted greedy or repair decoders. Others pass the neural output to an exact or heuristic solver, treating the solver as a black box that enforces feasibility or optimizes a modified objective. Differentiating through such a solver is difficult, so several methods use perturbation, smoothing, or implicit-gradient techniques to obtain usable learning signals \cite{berthet_learning_2020,vlastelica_differentiation_2019,blondel_efficient_2021}.

Perturb-and-MAP methods are especially relevant because they connect solver-in-the-loop learning with probabilistic modeling. By adding random perturbations to an energy or objective and repeatedly solving MAP problems, these methods define implicit distributions over structured outputs \cite{papandreou_perturb-and-map_2011,nowozin_perturb-and-map_2014,bertasius_local_2017,tarlow_randomized_2012}. They provide a bridge between optimization, sampling, and learning, but they rely on repeated optimization calls or randomized solver evaluations. The approach in this thesis instead uses differentiable approximate inference layers, such as LBP and NMF, to obtain structured marginals without calling a complete solver inside each training step.

\section{Graphical Models and Inference for Optimization}
\label{sec:rw_graphical_models}

Graphical models provide the most direct conceptual foundation for the thesis. In this view, a combinatorial problem is represented as an energy function or factor graph over discrete variables, and solving the problem corresponds to MAP inference, marginal inference, or sampling. Sections~\ref{sec:mrf_intro} and~\ref{subsec:background_approximate_inference} introduced MRFs and approximate inference; here we focus on how this perspective has been used in the literature and how it differs from the learned MRF models developed later.

\subsection{Combinatorial optimization and MAP inference}
\label{subsec:rw_co_as_map}

Many combinatorial optimization problems can be written as energy minimization problems over discrete configurations. Objective terms and constraints define local energy contributions, and low-energy states correspond to feasible or high-quality solutions. When the energy decomposes over variables and constraints, the resulting model can be represented as a factor graph or MRF. Classical inference methods such as max-product or max-sum belief propagation can then be interpreted as approximate MAP solvers \cite{kschischang_factor_2001,wainwright_graphical_2007}.

The connection also goes in the opposite direction. MAP inference in a discrete graphical model can be formulated as an integer linear program by introducing indicator variables for local configurations and enforcing marginal-consistency constraints. Its LP relaxation is closely related to the local polytope relaxation used in graphical-model inference. This equivalence is important for the thesis because it places BILP solving and MRF inference in a common optimization language: BILP constraints can define factors in an MRF, while MAP inference in such an MRF can itself be viewed as an integer-programming problem.

This perspective is particularly useful because it makes the dependency structure explicit. Factors encode which variables interact, and message passing propagates local compatibility information across the graph. Park and Shin show that a class of ILPs can be represented as MRFs and analyze conditions under which max-product belief propagation solves the corresponding optimization problem \cite{park_max-product_2017}. This is closely related to the canonical feasibility-cost MRF defined in Section~\ref{subsec:canonical-feasibility-cost-param} and used as the CMRF baseline in Chapter~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs}. However, the present work does not rely on exact convergence of max-product BP or on solving the MAP problem exactly. Instead, it uses finite-iteration marginal inference as a differentiable prediction layer whose outputs guide a decoder.

\subsection{Message passing, LP relaxations, and dual interpretations}
\label{subsec:rw_message_passing_duality}

The connection between MAP inference, linear programming relaxations, and dual decomposition is well established. Tree-reweighted message passing and related algorithms can be interpreted as optimizing bounds on the MAP objective or solving dual decompositions of graphical-model inference problems \cite{kolmogorov_convergent_2006,werner_linear_2007,komodakis_mrf_2007,komodakis_beyond_2009}. In these formulations, messages are not merely heuristic neural updates: they correspond to operations on a relaxation of a discrete optimization problem, such as coordinate updates, bound tightening, or dual-variable updates.

This literature matters for the present thesis for two reasons. First, it justifies treating message passing as an optimization-aware operation rather than as a generic graph neural architecture. Second, it clarifies what our method does not attempt to do. We do not use message passing to solve the MAP LP relaxation to optimality, nor do we claim that the resulting beliefs certify feasibility or optimality. Instead, Chapter~3 uses fixed-iteration LBP and NMF as differentiable maps from learned or canonical factor parameters to approximate variable or factor beliefs. These beliefs are then used as structured scores by a decoder. Thus, the factor graph structures the prediction layer, while feasibility of the final BILP assignment is still enforced by the decoding and propagation procedure.

\subsection{Learning energies and differentiating through inference}
\label{subsec:rw_learning_energies}

A related line of work learns the energy function or learns to approximate the inference process. In structured prediction energy networks and neural energy-based models, a learned energy assigns low values to desirable structured outputs and high values to implausible ones \cite{lecun_tutorial_2006,taskar_learning_2005,belanger_structured_2016}. Inference is then performed by MAP estimation, sampling, local search, or continuous relaxation. Other works learn to differentiate through optimization or approximate inference, enabling gradient-based training even when the prediction stage contains an optimization problem \cite{domke_learning_2013,amos_optnet_2017,donti_task-based_2017,wilder_end_2019,vlastelica_differentiation_2019,berthet_learning_2020,blondel_efficient_2021}.

Another direction learns the inference procedure itself. Inference machines learn iterative prediction rules for structured outputs \cite{ross_learning_2011}; learned message-passing methods parameterize or modify classical message updates \cite{heess_learning_2013}; amortized inference methods predict approximate marginals or Bethe fixed points directly \cite{wiseman_amortized_2019}. These methods show that classical inference algorithms can be embedded, unrolled, approximated, or replaced by neural computations.

The approach developed in Chapter~3 occupies a specific point in this landscape. It does not primarily learn new message-update equations. Instead, it keeps standard differentiable inference procedures fixed and learns the MRF parameters on which they operate. This separates the representation question from the inference question: the GNN predicts local factor tables, and LBP or NMF transforms those tables into structured beliefs used for training and decoding.

\section{Iterative Refinement and Generative Solvers}
\label{sec:rw_iterative_refinement}

The previous sections focused on solver guidance, direct prediction, and graphical-model inference. A more recent line of work views solution generation as an iterative refinement process. This perspective is relevant for Chapter~\ref{chapter:4_denoising_and_mrf_inference}, where solution prediction is formulated as learned refinement rather than one-shot variable prediction, and toward dynamics that progressively transform noisy, partial, relaxed, or low-quality assignments into better solutions.

\subsection{Sampling and classical stochastic search}
\label{subsec:rw_sampling_search}

Long before modern neural generative models, stochastic search methods interpreted optimization as exploration of an energy landscape. Simulated annealing samples from a Boltzmann distribution whose temperature is gradually reduced so that probability mass concentrates on low-energy states \cite{kirkpatrick_optimization_1983,hajek_cooling_1988}. Gibbs sampling and related Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods provide local stochastic dynamics for graphical models and discrete energy functions \cite{geman_stochastic_1984}. More broadly, many classical metaheuristics can be interpreted as stochastic or neighborhood-based exploration procedures over implicitly defined energy landscapes \cite{blum_metaheuristics_2003}.

These methods are not learned, but they provide an important conceptual bridge: a candidate solution can be improved by repeated local transitions guided by an energy, objective, or acceptance rule. Recent work has also revisited sampling-based CO in light of modern discrete MCMC and accelerator-friendly neighborhood exploration, showing that sampling remains relevant as a general-purpose optimization principle \cite{sun_revisiting_2023}.

\subsection{Denoising and diffusion models for CO solution representations}
\label{subsec:rw_diffusion_co}

Diffusion-style methods adapt generative modeling to combinatorial optimization by learning to reverse a corruption process over solution encodings. Most existing methods in this direction are not generic ILP or BILP solvers. Instead, they target specific CO representations, such as graphs, tours, cuts, or binary vectors associated with a particular problem family. DIFUSCO, for example, formulates NP-complete graph problems such as TSP and Max-Cut as binary-vector optimization tasks and trains graph neural networks to reverse Gaussian or Bernoulli corruption processes \cite{sun_difusco_2023}. Related work studies diffusion samplers for combinatorial optimization and statistical-physics-style energy landscapes \cite{sanokowski_diffusion_2024,sanokowski_scalable_2025}. These methods can represent multimodal distributions over high-quality solutions and generate diverse candidates, but high-quality sampling often requires many reverse steps and feasibility may still depend on decoding or repair.

Several recent works make diffusion-based solvers more efficient or more aligned with objective quality. Progressive distillation and fast transition-to-target methods reduce the number of denoising steps \cite{huang_accelerating_2023,li_t2t_2023,li_fast_2025}. DISCO constrains or simplifies the reverse process to improve efficiency \cite{zhao_disco_2025}. CADO uses reinforcement learning to fine-tune denoising policies toward optimization objectives \cite{yoon_cado_2024}. NEXCO and StruDiCO emphasize adaptive expansion or structure-preserving denoising, reflecting the idea that the reverse process should maintain and refine combinatorial structure rather than treat variables as independent reconstruction targets \cite{wang_native_2025,wang_strudico_2025}.

From the viewpoint of this thesis, diffusion solvers are relevant mainly because they frame solution generation as iterative refinement. They should not be presented as directly solving the same generic BILP prediction problem studied here. The connection is methodological: both diffusion solvers and our guided-decimation procedures refine partial or noisy solution information over time. The difference is that our refinement signal comes from approximate inference in an explicit MRF whose factor structure is derived from the BILP constraints. As in diffusion-based CO solvers, the final assignment still requires decoding and feasibility handling.

\subsection{Repair, local improvement, and test-time refinement}
\label{subsec:rw_repair_refinement}

Denoising is not limited to reversing an artificial noise process. In combinatorial optimization, a corrupted sample can also be interpreted as an infeasible, incomplete, or suboptimal assignment. Learned local improvement methods maintain an incumbent solution and repeatedly modify it by selecting neighborhoods, proposing moves, or rewriting substructures \cite{chen_learning_2019,garmendia_neural_2024,falkner_learning_2023}. Test-time adaptation and self-improvement methods further combine neural proposals with additional search, using generated solutions or improvement trajectories to adapt behavior on the specific instance being solved \cite{verdu_scaling_2024,pirnay_self-improvement_2024,li_generation_2025}.

This line of work is closely related to the decoding procedures used in this thesis. Guided decimation, for example, constructs a solution through a sequence of fixings and reinference steps. The important difference is that the refinement signal comes from conditional inference in an explicit factor graph: after part of the assignment is fixed, the MRF is conditioned on that evidence and the remaining marginals are recomputed. This provides a graphical-model interpretation of iterative solution construction.

\subsection{Flow-based and transport-based solution generation}
\label{subsec:rw_flow_co}

Flow-based generative models provide another refinement view: samples are transported from a simple source distribution toward a target data distribution by a learned velocity field. Flow matching trains such models by regressing target velocities along probability paths between noise and data samples \cite{lipman_flow_2022,tong_improving_2023}. Rectified flow learns approximately straight transport trajectories and can reduce the number of integration steps needed for generation \cite{liu_flow_2022}.

Adapting flow matching to combinatorial optimization raises the difficulty that classical flows are defined on continuous spaces, while feasible solutions are discrete. Discrete Flow Matching develops objectives directly over high-dimensional discrete state spaces \cite{gat_discrete_2024}, and related work studies more general discrete probability paths and continuous-time Markov-chain formulations \cite{shaul_flow_2024}. Reward-guided variants further optimize discrete flows toward task-specific objectives \cite{su_discrete_2026}. Recent CO methods use flow or rectified-flow dynamics as refinement operators over candidate solutions or solution representations \cite{li_generation_2025,friedmann_transport_2026}. As with diffusion-based solvers, these methods should be understood here as related generative principles rather than as generic BILP solvers.

Flow-based methods are useful for this thesis mainly as a modeling analogy: solving can be viewed as transporting a noisy, relaxed, partial, or suboptimal assignment toward a high-quality discrete solution. This principle is compatible with graphical-model inference, where iterative updates transform local potentials and evidence into beliefs over remaining variables. Later chapters build on this connection while keeping the BILP-specific distinction explicit: the generated or inferred information is used to guide a decoder, and feasibility is handled by the decoding and propagation mechanism.

\section{Research Gap and Thesis Positioning}
\label{sec:rw_gap_positioning}

The literature reviewed above suggests that learning for ILP, MILP, and related combinatorial optimization problems has advanced along several complementary directions. Solver-guidance methods learn to control exact or heuristic algorithms. Neural construction methods learn variable scores or sequential policies. Graphical-model methods provide explicit dependency models and inference algorithms. Generative and refinement methods view solution construction as an iterative transformation. The gap addressed by this thesis lies at the intersection of these directions, but with a specific scope: BILP solution prediction. We study how explicit MRF-style dependency modeling can serve as the structured layer through which learned predictions are transformed into marginals or scores for binary decision variables.

These observations lead to three concrete gaps that motivate the following chapters. First, many neural ILP predictors operate through factorized variable scores. Although the input representation may encode the variable--constraint graph, the output distribution does not explicitly represent dependencies between variables. Chapter~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs} addresses this gap by replacing independent Bernoulli predictions with MRF-structured distributions and differentiable approximate inference. The resulting marginals are not final feasible solutions; they are structured decoding signals over BILP variables.

Second, iterative generative solvers show that solution construction can be viewed as refinement rather than one-shot prediction. However, in much of this literature the dependencies between variables are represented implicitly through neural architectures. Chapter~\ref{chapter:4_denoising_and_mrf_inference} builds on the refinement view, while Chapter~\ref{chapter:5_feature-to-factor_mappings_for_approximate_mrf_inference} addresses the scalability of the factor-parameter mappings needed by such structured models. This does not remove the need for decoding or feasibility handling. Rather, it changes the information used by the decoder: instead of relying only on independent neural scores, the decoder is guided by approximate marginals obtained from an MRF conditioned on the current partial assignment.

Third, exact over-complete factor tables and repeated inference become expensive for high-arity constraints. This motivates approximate factor-graph parameterizations and scalable learned inference mechanisms that retain the benefits of structured probabilistic modeling while reducing the cost of direct MRF inference.
