\section{Chapter overview}

This thesis studies how machine learning can be combined with structured inference to support the solution of integer linear programs (ILPs). The problems considered in Chapters~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs}--\ref{chapter:5_feature-to-factor_mappings_for_approximate_mrf_inference} are combinatorial: feasible solutions are discrete, highly constrained, and often expensive to search over directly. For this reason, the thesis draws on three complementary areas. Integer linear programming provides the formal optimization setting; machine learning provides tools for learning from families of problem instances; and Markov Random Fields (MRFs) provide a probabilistic language for representing dependencies among discrete decision variables.

This chapter introduces the background needed to connect these areas. We first review ILPs, their formal definition, and the main algorithmic ideas behind modern solution methods, with particular attention to the components where learned predictions may be useful. We then introduce the machine-learning concepts used in the learned MRF predictors of Chapter~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs}, the refinement models of Chapter~\ref{chapter:4_denoising_and_mrf_inference}, and the feature-to-factor mappings of Chapter~\ref{chapter:5_feature-to-factor_mappings_for_approximate_mrf_inference}, including neural networks and graph neural networks, which are well suited to data defined on combinatorial structures. Finally, we present MRFs, energy-based models, and approximate inference methods, since these provide the structured probabilistic framework used to transform local learned information into globally coherent predictions.
