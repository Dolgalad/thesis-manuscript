\section{Markov Random Fields}
\label{sec:mrf_intro}

\subsection{Boltzmann distributions}

Boltzmann, or Gibbs, distributions define probabilities through an energy function. For a configuration $x \in \mathcal{X}$ and energy $E(x)$, the Boltzmann distribution at inverse temperature $\beta = 1/T$ is
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:boltzmann_distribution}
    p_\beta(x) = \frac{1}{Z(\beta)} \exp\!\left(-\beta E(x)\right), \qquad 
    Z(\beta) = \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}} \exp\!\left(-\beta E(x)\right).
\end{equation}
The partition function $Z(\beta)$ normalizes the distribution, but computing it requires summing over all configurations. This is generally intractable for high-dimensional discrete spaces.

Boltzmann distributions are useful for connecting probabilistic modeling with optimization. As $\beta \to 0$, the distribution approaches the uniform distribution over $\mathcal{X}$. As $\beta \to \infty$, probability mass concentrates on configurations that minimize $E(x)$. Thus, when feasible assignments are encoded in the state space or through hard or soft potentials, low-energy states correspond to high-quality combinatorial solutions. This connection underlies classical methods such as simulated annealing \citep{kirkpatrick_optimization_1983} and motivates the use of energy-based graphical models in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Exponential-family form}
A large class of energy-based models, including Markov Random Fields (MRFs), can be expressed within the general framework of exponential families. In this setting, a distribution over configurations $x \in \mathcal{X}$ is written as

\begin{equation}
p_\GENEPARAM(x) = \exp\!\left(\GENEPARAM^\top \SSTAT(x) - A(\GENEPARAM)\right),
\end{equation}

where $\SSTAT(x)$ is a vector of sufficient statistics, $\GENEPARAM$ is the vector of natural parameters, and $A(\GENEPARAM)=\log \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}} \exp(\GENEPARAM^\top \SSTAT(x))$ is the log-partition function. This formulation generalizes the Boltzmann distribution by emphasizing the role of feature functions $\SSTAT(x)$, which capture local interactions or structural properties of $x$. The log-partition function $A(\GENEPARAM)$ ensures normalization and is also the cumulant-generating function of the sufficient statistics. Its convexity is central to the theory of exponential-family learning, although maximum-likelihood estimation remains computationally challenging in MRFs because the required model expectations are generally intractable \citep{wainwright_graphical_2007}. In this convention, $\GENEPARAM^\top\SSTAT(x)$ is an unnormalized log-probability, or score. Equivalently, if one writes the model in Boltzmann form, then $E(x)=-\GENEPARAM^\top\SSTAT(x)$ up to an additive constant. This sign convention is used later when learned MRF parameters are interpreted as defining negative energies.

\paragraph{Variational characterization of the log-partition}

A useful property of exponential-family models is that the log-partition function admits a variational characterization. Let
\begin{equation}
    \mu = \mathbb{E}_{p}[\SSTAT(X)]
\end{equation}
denote the vector of mean parameters, and let $\MARGINALP$ be the \emph{marginal polytope}, defined as the set of all mean-parameter vectors that can arise from some probability distribution over $\mathcal{X}$. Writing $\Delta(\mathcal{X})$ for the probability simplex over $\mathcal{X}$, we define
\begin{equation}
    \MARGINALP = \left\{
        \mu \in \mathbb{R}^d : \exists q \in \Delta(\mathcal{X}) \ \text{such that}\ \mu = \mathbb{E}_{q}[\SSTAT(X)]
    \right\}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, in a discrete graphical model with indicator sufficient statistics, $\MARGINALP$ is the set of all collections of unary and factor marginals that are globally consistent with some joint distribution.

The log-partition function can then be written as the solution of the variational optimization problem
\begin{equation}
    A(\GENEPARAM) = \sup_{\mu\in\MARGINALP} \left\{ \GENEPARAM^\top \mu + H(\mu) \right\},
\end{equation}
where $H(\mu)$ denotes the entropy of a joint distribution realizing the mean parameters $\mu$. For finite discrete models, this supremum is attained when $\MARGINALP$ is taken to be the closed marginal polytope. In energy notation, with $E(x)=-\GENEPARAM^\top\SSTAT(x)$, this becomes
\begin{equation}
    \log Z = \sup_{\mu\in\MARGINALP} \left\{ H(\mu) - \mathbb{E}_{\mu}[E(X)] \right\}.
\end{equation}
This formulation shows that the log-partition function is not merely a normalizing constant: it is the convex dual object that relates natural parameters $\GENEPARAM$ to mean parameters \(\mu\). This relationship is central to maximum-likelihood learning, because learning depends on matching empirical sufficient statistics to model expectations.

Classical maximum-likelihood estimation in exponential-family models is often described through moment matching. For a dataset $\DATASET=\{x^{(1)},\dots,x^{(N)}\}$, the gradient of the log-likelihood contains an empirical term and a model-expectation term:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\GENEPARAM \log p_\GENEPARAM(\mathcal{D}) 
= 
\sum_{i=1}^N \SSTAT(x^{(i)}) 
- 
N \,\mathbb{E}_{p_\GENEPARAM}[\SSTAT(X)] .
\end{equation}
The empirical sufficient statistics are directly available from data, but the model expectation is generally intractable because it depends on the normalized distribution. This difficulty motivates the approximate inference methods introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:background_approximate_inference}. From the variational perspective, these methods approximate the exact optimization problem defining $A(\GENEPARAM)$, either by restricting the family of admissible distributions, by replacing the marginal polytope with a simpler outer approximation, or by approximating the entropy term.

\subsubsection{Sampling from Boltzmann distributions}

Sampling methods provide a classical approach to approximate inference in Boltzmann distributions and MRFs. Since the partition function is usually intractable, these methods construct a stochastic process whose stationary distribution is the target distribution, and then approximate expectations using samples from that process. The most common examples are Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods such as Metropolis--Hastings (MH) and Gibbs sampling \citep{metropolis_monte_1949,hastings_monte_1970,geman_stochastic_1984}.

Metropolis--Hastings proposes a new state $\XVEC'$ from a proposal distribution $\PROPOSAL(\XVEC'|\XVEC)$ and accepts it with probability
\begin{equation}
\alpha(\XVEC,\XVEC')
=
\min\!\left(
1,
\frac{p_\beta(\XVEC')\PROPOSAL(\XVEC|\XVEC')}{p_\beta(\XVEC)\PROPOSAL(\XVEC'|\XVEC)}
\right).
\end{equation}
For a symmetric proposal this reduces to an acceptance rule depending only on the energy difference, so lower-energy moves are always accepted and higher-energy moves are accepted with probability controlled by the temperature. This makes MH very general, but its efficiency depends strongly on the proposal distribution.

Gibbs sampling is especially natural for MRFs. Instead of proposing a full new configuration, it repeatedly samples one variable from its conditional distribution given the current values of the others:
\begin{equation}
p(x_i \mid x_{-i})
=
p(x_i \mid x_{N(i)})
\propto
\prod_{\FACTOR \ni i} \POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}).
\end{equation}
The second equality follows from the local Markov structure of the MRF: once the neighboring variables are fixed, the conditional distribution of $x_i$ depends only on factors incident to $i$. This locality makes Gibbs sampling simple to implement for sparse graphical models and useful as a baseline or diagnostic sampling method.

However, MCMC methods can mix slowly in high-dimensional discrete spaces, especially for combinatorial optimization problems whose energy landscapes are rugged and multimodal. Low-energy configurations may be sparse and separated by high-energy barriers, so single-variable updates can require many iterations to explore the relevant parts of the distribution. For this reason, sampling is useful background for the thesis, but the main inference mechanisms developed later are deterministic differentiable approximations such as Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP) and Naive Mean-Field inference (NMF).

\subsection{Graphical models}

Graphical models represent high-dimensional probability distributions by decomposing them into local functions associated with a graph. In this thesis, we focus on undirected graphical models, or Markov Random Fields, because they provide a natural way to define energies over structured binary assignments. Local potentials encode interactions among small groups of variables, while the global distribution is obtained by combining these local terms. Factor graphs make this decomposition explicit and provide the computational structure used by message-passing inference algorithms.

\subsubsection{Markov random fields}

MRFs, also known as undirected graphical models or Gibbs random fields, represent structured probability distributions through local interactions. Let $G=(V,E)$ be an undirected graph, where each vertex $v\in V$ corresponds to a random variable $X_v$, and let $X=(X_v)_{v\in V}$ denote the collection of variables. The graph encodes the local Markov property:
\begin{equation}
X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_{V\setminus(\{i\}\cup N(i))}\mid X_{N(i)} .
\end{equation}
That is, once the neighboring variables of $X_i$ are known, variables outside this neighborhood do not directly affect the conditional distribution of $X_i$.

For strictly positive distributions, the Hammersley--Clifford theorem connects this conditional-independence view to a factorized representation over cliques of the graph \citep{besag_spatial_1974,grimmett_theorem_1973,grimmett_disorder_1990}. In particular, an MRF can be written as
\begin{equation}
p(x)=\frac{1}{Z}\prod_{c\in\mathcal{C}(G)}\POTENTIAL_c(x_c),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}(G)$ denotes the cliques of $G$, $\POTENTIAL_c(x_c)$ is a nonnegative potential function, and $Z$ is the partition function. Equivalently, defining local energies $E_c(x_c)=-\log \POTENTIAL_c(x_c)$ gives
\begin{equation}
p(x)=\frac{1}{Z}\exp\!\left(-\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}(G)}E_c(x_c)\right).
\end{equation}
This factorized form is the representation used throughout the thesis.

For MRFs, the exponential-family parameterization arises naturally when the clique potentials are expressed as
\begin{equation}
\POTENTIAL_c(x_c;\GENEPARAM_c) = \exp\big(\GENEPARAM_c^\top \SSTAT_c(x_c)\big).
\end{equation}
In this case, the resulting distribution takes the exponential-family form
\begin{equation}
p(x;\GENEPARAM)=\frac{1}{Z(\GENEPARAM)}\exp\!\Big(\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}(G)} \GENEPARAM_c^\top \SSTAT_c(x_c)\Big),
\end{equation}
where the global feature vector $\SSTAT(x)$ decomposes as a sum of local clique features and $\GENEPARAM = (\GENEPARAM_c)_{c\in \mathcal{C}(G)}$ are the parameters of the distribution. In this formulation, the graph structure determines the decomposition of the sufficient statistics, while the parameters control the strength of local interactions.

The important point for this thesis is that the same exponential-family form can represent both independent binary variables and structured graphical models. In an MRF, the sufficient statistics decompose over variables and factors, and the associated parameters define local potentials.

The expressiveness of MRFs makes them well suited for modeling structured domains where local interactions govern global behavior. However, inference in general MRFs is computationally challenging. Computing the partition function $Z$ requires summing over all possible configurations of $X$, which grows exponentially with the number of variables. Marginal inference and partition-function computation are \#P-hard in general, while MAP inference is NP-hard \citep{chandrasekaran_complexity_2008,schrijver_combinatorial_2003}. This motivates approximate inference algorithms such as belief propagation, variational methods, and sampling techniques.

In the context of this thesis, MRFs serve as the core formalism for linking energy-based models to combinatorial optimization. By designing potential functions $\POTENTIAL_c$ that encode local constraints and objective terms, BILP objectives and constraints can be represented as energies or potentials over binary assignments, with low-energy states corresponding to high-quality solutions. In this work, we consider parameterized MRF distributions where the factor structure is derived from the optimization instance and the local potentials are learned from data.

\subsubsection{Factor graphs}

Factor graphs provide an explicit representation of the factorization structure of a graphical model. A factor graph is a bipartite graph 
$\FG=(\FGV\cup\FGF,\FGE)$, where $\FGV$ denotes variable nodes and $\FGF$ denotes factor nodes. Each variable node corresponds to a random variable, and each factor node corresponds to a local potential. An edge $(i,\FACTOR)\in \FGE$ indicates that variable $i$ participates in factor $\FACTOR$. The joint distribution then factorizes as
\begin{equation}
p(x) = \frac{1}{Z}\prod_{\FACTOR\in \FGF}\POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}),
\end{equation}
where $x_{\FACTOR}$ denotes the local assignment of the variables in the scope of factor $\FACTOR$.

We write $N(\FACTOR)$ for the set of variables incident to factor $\FACTOR$, and define the arity of the factor as
\begin{equation*}
|\FACTOR| \coloneqq |N(\FACTOR)|.
\end{equation*}
Thus, unary, pairwise, and higher-order factors correspond to $|\FACTOR|=1$, $|\FACTOR|=2$, and $|\FACTOR|>2$, respectively. 

For a global assignment $\XVEC$, we write
\begin{equation*}
    \XVEC_{\FACTOR} = (\XVEC_i)_{i\in N(\FACTOR)}
\end{equation*}
for the restriction of $\XVEC$ to the variables in the scope of factor $\FACTOR$. For $i\in N(\FACTOR)$, we write
\begin{equation*}
    \XVEC_{\FACTOR\setminus i} = (\XVEC_j)_{j\in N(\FACTOR)\setminus\{i\}}
\end{equation*}
for the assignment of all variables in the factor scope except $i$. Accordingly, a sum such as
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{\XVEC_{\FACTOR\setminus i}}
\end{equation*}
ranges over all assignments to the variables in $N(\FACTOR)\setminus\{i\}$ for each fixed value of $\XVEC_i$.

This representation is closely related to, but distinct from, the variable--constraint graphs introduced earlier for BILPs. A variable--constraint graph represents the input structure of an optimization instance and is used as the graph on which neural encoders operate. A factor graph represents a probabilistic model over solution variables and is used for inference. In many cases the two graphs have similar bipartite structure, because ILP constraints naturally suggest factor scopes, but they play different roles.

Factor graphs are especially useful for message-passing inference. Algorithms such as sum--product and max--product pass messages along variable--factor edges, separating variable updates from factor updates. In combinatorial optimization, local constraints such as edge-covering constraints in vertex cover, exclusion constraints in independent set, or coverage constraints in set cover can be encoded as factors that assign high potential to locally compatible assignments and low potential to incompatible ones.

\paragraph{Canonical over-complete representation}
For discrete graphical models, an equivalent but often more convenient parameterization is obtained through the canonical over-complete representation. Rather than associating features with a minimal set of sufficient statistics, one introduces indicator functions for every local assignment of each variable and factor. For a factor graph with variables $X_i \in \mathcal{X}_i$ for $i\in\FGV$ and factors $\FACTOR \in \FGF$, the unary sufficient statistics are
\begin{equation}
\SSTAT_{i,y}(x)=\mathbbm{1}\{x_i=y\}, \qquad y\in\mathcal{X}_i,
\end{equation}
while the factor sufficient statistics are
\begin{equation}
\SSTAT_{\FACTOR,y_{\FACTOR}}(\XVEC)=\mathbbm{1}\{\XFACT=y_{\FACTOR}\}, \qquad y_{\FACTOR}\in\mathcal{X}_{\FACTOR} .
\end{equation}
The corresponding exponential-family distribution can then be written as
\begin{equation}
p_{\GENEPARAM}(\XVEC)
=
\exp\left(
\sum_{i\in \FGV}\sum_{y\in\mathcal{X}_i}\GENEPARAM_{i,y}\SSTAT_{i,y}(\XVEC)
+
\sum_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}\sum_{y_{\FACTOR}\in\mathcal{X}_{\FACTOR}}
\GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR,y_{\FACTOR}}\SSTAT_{\FACTOR,y_{\FACTOR}}(\XVEC)
-
A(\GENEPARAM)
\right).
\end{equation}
This representation is called over-complete because the indicator statistics are linearly dependent: for each variable or factor, exactly one local configuration is active, so the associated indicators sum to one. Consequently, different parameter vectors can define the same distribution, so the parameters are not uniquely identifiable unless one imposes a convention for removing this redundancy. Nevertheless, the over-complete form is useful for graphical models because it aligns directly with local potentials and marginal inference. In this parameterization, the expected value of a unary indicator is the corresponding unary marginal, and the expected value of a factor indicator is the corresponding factor marginal. Approximate inference algorithms such as belief propagation therefore naturally operate on these quantities, returning beliefs $\BELIEF_i(x_i)$ and $\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})$ that approximate the marginal expectations of the corresponding indicator sufficient statistics \citep{wainwright_graphical_2007}.

\paragraph{Instance-conditioned parameterization}
The distributions used later in this thesis should be understood as conditional MRF models over solution variables, conditioned on the data of a combinatorial optimization instance. That is, for a problem instance $\PROB$, the random variables are the candidate solution variables $\XVEC$, while the instance data are treated as fixed input information. The dependence on $\PROB$ enters through the factor graph structure and through the local natural parameters. In this sense, the model used later can be written informally as
\begin{equation*}
    p_{\GENEPARAM(\PROB)}(\XVEC \mid \PROB),
\end{equation*}
where $\GENEPARAM(\PROB)$ denotes the collection of local factor parameters associated with the instance $\PROB$. When the instance is fixed or clear from context, we suppress the conditioning notation and write simply $p_{\GENEPARAM}(\XVEC)$. Thus, throughout the thesis, references to an MRF over a BILP instance should be read as references to this instance-conditioned undirected model.

For binary variables, the size of an over-complete factor table grows exponentially with the arity of the factor. If factor $\FACTOR$ has arity $|\FACTOR|$, then its local assignment space has size
\begin{equation*}
|\mathcal{X}_{\FACTOR}| = 2^{|\FACTOR|}.
\end{equation*}
Thus, representing a separate parameter for every local assignment requires $2^{|\FACTOR|}$ factor parameters. The same exponential dependence appears in exact factor-to-variable message updates: for a binary factor $\FACTOR$, computing one outgoing message generally requires summing or maximizing over $2^{|\FACTOR|-1}$ assignments of the remaining variables in the factor scope. This scaling is manageable for unary and pairwise factors but becomes problematic for high-arity constraints, such as set-cover constraints involving many variables. This limitation motivates the approximate factor-graph constructions studied later in the thesis.

\subsubsection{Canonical feasibility-cost parameterization for BILPs}
\label{subsec:canonical-feasibility-cost-param}
In \citep{park_max-product_2017} the authors show that certain ILPs can be solved efficiently using the Max-Product algorithm. They define a potential for each constraint that encodes local feasibility and assigns $0$ probability to all infeasible assignments. In the following we derive what we call the \emph{feasibility-cost parameterization}, a parameterization for the canonical-over complete representation of the same model. 

We now define a fixed, non-learned choice of the generic parameters $\GENEPARAM$ introduced above. This parameterization maps an integer linear program to an MRF by encoding feasibility through constraint factors and objective value through unary factors. Local assignments that satisfy a constraint receive larger log-potential values than assignments that violate it, thereby increasing the probability of globally feasible assignments.

We refer to the resulting fixed model as the \emph{canonical Markov random field} (CMRF), and to its parameter choice as the \emph{canonical feasibility-cost parameterization}. We denote the corresponding parameters by $\CANEPARAM$.

Let $\XVEC = (\XVEC_1,\ldots,\XVEC_{\NVAR})$ denote the binary decision variables of an ILP. Each constraint factor $\FACTOR \in \FGF$ is associated with a constraint of the ILP and has scope $N(\FACTOR) \subseteq \{ 1,\ldots, \NVAR\}$. We write $\XVEC_{\FACTOR} = (\XVEC_i)_{i \in N(\FACTOR)}$ for a local assignment to the variables appearing in constraint $\FACTOR$. The set of locally feasible assignments for this constraint is denoted by 
\begin{equation}
    \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR}
    =
    \{\XVEC_{\FACTOR} : \XVEC_{\FACTOR}
    \text{ satisfies constraint } \FACTOR\}.
\end{equation}

The corresponding MRF constraint potential is the feasibility indicator
\begin{equation}
    \POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(\XFACT)
    =
    \begin{cases}
        1, & \XVEC_{\FACTOR} \in \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR},\\
        0, & \XVEC_{\FACTOR} \notin \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Unary factors encode the contribution of individual variables to the objective. For a maximization problem with objective $\sum_{i=1}^{\NVAR} c_i \XVEC_i$, the unary potential can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \POTENTIAL_i(\XVEC_i) = \exp(c_i \XVEC_i).
\end{equation}

In the over-complete exponential-family representation, each factor has one parameter for each possible local configuration. We therefore define the
constraint-factor log-potentials by 
\begin{equation}
    \CANEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(\XFACT)
    =
    \begin{cases}
        0, & \XFACT \in \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR},\\
        -\infty, & \XFACT \notin \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR},
    \end{cases}
    \qquad \FACTOR \in \FGF,
\end{equation}
and the unary log-potentials by
\begin{equation}
    \CANEPARAM_i(\XVEC_i) = c_i \XVEC_i.
\end{equation}
The resulting MRF defines the unnormalized distribution
\begin{equation}
    p_{\CANEPARAM}(\XVEC)
    \propto
    \exp \left(
        \sum_{i=1}^{\NVAR} \CANEPARAM_i(\XVEC_i)
        +
        \sum_{\FACTOR \in \FGF} \CANEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(\XFACT)
    \right).
\end{equation}

Under this parameterization, every globally infeasible assignment receives zero probability mass, since it violates at least one constraint factor and therefore obtains log-potential $-\infty$. Among feasible assignments, the probability mass is proportional to the exponential of the ILP objective value. Under exact MAP inference in the hard-constraint model, the maximizer of the MRF probability therefore recovers an optimal feasible solution with respect to the encoded score:
\begin{equation}
    \argmax_{\XVEC} p_{\CANEPARAM}(\XVEC)
    =
    \argmax_{\XVEC \in \mathcal{X}}
    \sum_{i=1}^{\NVAR} c_i \XVEC_i,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{X}$ denotes the global feasible set.

In practice, using $-\infty$ is numerically inconvenient. We therefore use a large finite penalty $M \gg 0$ and replace the hard infeasibility parameter by
\begin{equation}
    \CANEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(\XFACT)
    =
    \begin{cases}
        0, & \XFACT \in \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR},\\
        -M, & \XFACT \notin \FEASIBLESET_{\FACTOR}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The finite value $-M$ is a numerical approximation to the hard infeasibility penalty $-\infty$. It assigns infeasible local configurations very small, rather than zero, probability mass. Larger values of $M$ make the finite model closer to the hard-constraint model, but do not make the constraint exact unless the limit $M \to \infty$ is taken.

The canonical feasibility-cost parameterization provides a direct over-complete MRF representation of the ILP. It is fixed by the constraint structure and objective coefficients, and therefore does not involve learned parameters. Its parameter vector $\CANEPARAM(\PROB)$ is constructed directly from the BILP constraints and objective, and contains no learned factor parameters.

\subsection{Approximate inference}
\label{subsec:background_approximate_inference}

In graphical models, \emph{inference} refers to the set of tasks through which one extracts useful probabilistic information from a factorized distribution. The most common forms are: computing marginal distributions of individual variables or subsets of variables, estimating conditional probabilities given evidence, evaluating the partition function, and solving for the maximum a posteriori (MAP) configuration. Each of these problems is central to applications of Markov Random Fields: marginals provide uncertainty estimates, the partition function controls normalization and likelihood evaluation, and MAP inference directly corresponds to combinatorial optimization by identifying the lowest-energy configuration.

Exact inference is only tractable for models with restricted structure, such as trees or graphs with small treewidth. For general graphs, marginal inference and partition-function computation are \#P-hard, while MAP inference is NP-hard \citep{chandrasekaran_complexity_2008,schrijver_combinatorial_2003}. As a result, approximate inference is needed. This thesis focuses on two deterministic iterative methods: Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP) and Naive Mean-Field (NMF) inference. Both can be run for a fixed number of iterations and treated as differentiable computation graphs.

For this thesis, inference plays a dual role. It provides approximate marginal information for decoding, and it also acts as a differentiable structured layer through which gradients can be backpropagated. This integration of structured inference with neural prediction sets the stage for the contributions developed in later chapters.


\subsubsection{Sum--product belief propagation}
\label{subsec:background_lbp}
The sum--product algorithm, also known as Belief Propagation (BP), is a message-passing procedure for computing marginal distributions in factor graphs. Its central idea is that local computations on the bipartite structure of the factor graph can be combined to produce beliefs over variables and factors. On tree-structured graphs, the algorithm is exact and runs in time linear in the number of edges; on graphs with cycles it becomes an approximation, known as \emph{loopy BP} \citep{yedidia_understanding_2003,kschischang_factor_2001}.

Formally, let $\FG=(\FGV\cup\FGF,\FGE)$ be a factor graph. Messages are passed iteratively along edges from variables to factors and from factors to variables:
\begin{align}
    \label{equ:bp_variable_to_factor_message}
    m_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}(x_i) &= \prod_{\FACTOR'\in N(i)\setminus\{\FACTOR\}} m_{\FACTOR'\rightarrow i}(x_i),\\
    \label{equ:bp_factor_to_variable_message}
    m_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}(x_i) &= \sum_{x_{\FACTOR\setminus i}} \POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})\prod_{j\in N(\FACTOR)\setminus\{i\}} m_{j\rightarrow \FACTOR}(x_j).
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{equ:bp_variable_to_factor_message} collects information arriving at a variable from all neighboring factors except the recipient factor. Equation~\eqref{equ:bp_factor_to_variable_message} aggregates over the local configurations of the factor that are consistent with the target variable state (see Figure~\ref{fig:bp_message_passing}). Once messages have converged, or after a fixed number of iterations, beliefs are computed as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:bp_beliefs}
    \BELIEF_i(x_i)\propto \prod_{\FACTOR\in N(i)} m_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}(x_i), \qquad \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})\propto \POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) \prod_{i\in N(\FACTOR)} m_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}(x_i).
\end{equation}
The term \emph{belief} is standard in the BP literature and refers to the algorithm's current estimate of a marginal distribution.Thus, $\BELIEF_i(x_i)$ is the approximate unary marginal assigned to state $x_i$, and $\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})$ is the approximate factor marginal assigned to local configuration $x_{\FACTOR}$. On trees these beliefs coincide exactly with the true marginals; on loopy graphs they should be interpreted as locally normalized approximate marginals produced by the message-passing iterations.

For numerical stability and implementation efficiency, the algorithms used in this thesis are written in the log-potential domain. For each factor $\FACTOR$, let $\GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}$ denote the factor's log-potential table, with entries indexed by local assignments:
\begin{equation}
\label{equ:log_potential_definition}
    \GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) \coloneq \log \POTENTIAL_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) = -E_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}).
\end{equation}
Thus, $\EPARAM_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})$ denotes the log-potential value assigned to local configuration $x_{\FACTOR}$. Larger values correspond to lower local energy and higher local compatibility.

The factor-to-variable message is computed as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:lbp_log_factor_to_variable}
    m_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x_i) = \log \sum_{x_{\FACTOR\setminus i}}
    \exp\left(
        \GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})
        +
        \sum_{j\in N(\FACTOR)\setminus\{i\}}
        m_{j\rightarrow \FACTOR}^{(t)}(x_j)
    \right).
\end{equation}
The undamped variable-to-factor message is then
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:lbp_log_variable_to_factor}
    \widetilde{m}_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}^{(t+1)}(x_i)
    =
    \sum_{\FACTOR'\in N(i)\setminus\{\FACTOR\}}
    m_{\FACTOR'\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x_i)
    -
    \log
    \sum_{x'_i}
    \exp\left(
        \sum_{\FACTOR'\in N(i)\setminus\{\FACTOR\}}
        m_{\FACTOR'\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x'_i)
    \right).
\end{equation}

In practice, we use damped message updates. Given a damping coefficient
$\BPDAMPING\in[0,1]$, the updated message is
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:lbp_damping}
    m_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}^{(t+1)}
    =
    \BPDAMPING m_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}^{(t)}
    +
    (1-\BPDAMPING)
    \widetilde{m}_{i\rightarrow \FACTOR}^{(t+1)} .
\end{equation}
Damping mixes the previous message with the newly computed message to stabilize loopy BP iterations; beliefs are computed from the final damped messages.

Equations~\eqref{equ:lbp_log_factor_to_variable}--\eqref{equ:lbp_damping} give the log-domain LBP updates used later in the thesis. Each factor-to-variable message depends only on the factor log-potential table and incoming messages from the other variables in that factor scope, making BP naturally parallelizable.

The log-messages are initialized to zero. In the probability domain, this corresponds to initializing all messages uniformly, since exponentiating a zero log-message gives an uninformative constant message. This neutral initialization is convenient when BP is used as a fixed-iteration differentiable inference layer, because it does not inject any preference before the learned factor log-potentials are used.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
    \caption{Sum--product Belief Propagation}
    \label{alg:sum_product_bp}
    \KwData{Factor graph $\FG=(\FGV\cup\FGF,\FGE)$, log-potentials $\{\GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}\}_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}$, number of iterations $T$, damping coefficient $\BPDAMPING\in[0,1]$}
    \KwResult{Approximate beliefs $\{\BELIEF_i\}_{i\in\FGV}$ and $\{\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}\}_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}$}
    Initialize all variable-to-factor log-messages $m_{i\to \FACTOR}^{(0)}(x_i)=0$, for $(i,\FACTOR)\in\FGE$ and all states $x_i$\;
    \For{$t=1,\dots,T$}{
        \ForEach{$(i,\FACTOR)\in\FGE$}{
            Compute $m_{\FACTOR\to i}^{(t)}$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:lbp_log_factor_to_variable}\;
            Compute $\widetilde{m}_{i\to \FACTOR}^{(t+1)}$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:lbp_log_variable_to_factor}\;
            Update $m_{i\to \FACTOR}^{(t+1)}$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:lbp_damping}\;
        }
    }
    Compute beliefs $\BELIEF_i$ and $\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}$ from the final log-messages by normalizing the corresponding log-beliefs\;
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Bethe free energy and approximate marginals}
The variational view introduced in the exponential-family discussion provides another interpretation of BP. Exact computation of the log-partition function can be expressed as an entropy-regularized optimization problem over the marginal polytope. The Bethe approximation makes this problem tractable by replacing the exact marginal polytope with a set of locally consistent beliefs and by replacing the exact joint entropy with a locally decomposed entropy approximation.

Let $\BELIEF_i(x_i)$ denote a belief over variable $i$, and let $\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})$ denote a belief over the variables adjacent to factor $\FACTOR$. These beliefs are required to satisfy normalization constraints
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{x_i} \BELIEF_i(x_i) = 1, \qquad \sum_{x_{\FACTOR}} \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) = 1,
\end{equation}
and local consistency constraints
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{x_{\FACTOR\setminus i}} \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) = \BELIEF_i(x_i), \qquad \text{for all } i\in N(\FACTOR).
\end{equation}
The set of beliefs satisfying these constraints is called the \emph{local polytope}, denoted by $\LOCALP$. It is easier to describe than the marginal polytope because it enforces consistency only between variables and their neighboring factors, rather than requiring that all local beliefs arise from a globally consistent joint distribution.

Let $d_i = |N(i)|$ be the degree of variable \(i\) in the factor graph. The Bethe entropy is defined as
\begin{equation}
    H_{\mathrm{Bethe}}(\BELIEF) = \sum_{\FACTOR\in\FGF} H(\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}) - \sum_{i\in\FGV} (d_i - 1) H(\BELIEF_i),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    H(\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}) = -\sum_{x_{\FACTOR}} \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) \log \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}), \qquad H(\BELIEF_i) = -\sum_{x_i} \BELIEF_i(x_i) \log \BELIEF_i(x_i).
\end{equation}
The correction term subtracts repeated counts of variable entropies that appear across neighboring factor beliefs.

The Bethe free energy is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Bethe}}(\BELIEF) = \sum_{\FACTOR\in\FGF} \sum_{x_{\FACTOR}} \BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) E_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR}) - H_{\mathrm{Bethe}}(\BELIEF).
\end{equation}
The Bethe approximation to the log-partition function is obtained from
\begin{equation}
    \log Z \approx -\min_{\BELIEF\in\LOCALP} \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Bethe}}(\BELIEF).
\end{equation}
If $\BELIEF^\star$ is a stationary point of this constrained problem, then
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\log Z}_{\mathrm{Bethe}} = -\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{Bethe}}(\BELIEF^\star).
\end{equation}
Stationary points of the Bethe free energy correspond to fixed points of loopy BP \citep{yedidia_understanding_2003}. Thus, the beliefs produced by message passing can be interpreted as approximate marginals, while the value of the Bethe free energy at those beliefs provides an approximation to the log-partition function.

\subsubsection{Mean--field inference}
\label{subsec:background_mean_field}

Mean-Field (MF) methods approximate an intractable distribution $p(\XVEC)$ by a distribution $q(\XVEC)$ from a tractable family $\mathcal{Q}$. The approximation is commonly chosen by minimizing the Kullback--Leibler divergence
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:mean_field_kl}
    q^\star
    =
    \argmin_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \KL(q(\XVEC)\,\|\,p(\XVEC)).
\end{equation}
Equivalently, for an MRF with energy $E(\XVEC)=\sum_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}E_{\FACTOR}(\XVEC_{\FACTOR})$ and $p(\XVEC)\propto \exp(-E(\XVEC))$, this is equivalent to maximizing the variational objective
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:mean_field_objective}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MF}}(q)
    =
    H(q)
    -
    \mathbb{E}_{q}[E(\XVEC)] ,
\end{equation}
where $H(q)$ is the entropy of the approximate distribution. Different choices of $\mathcal{Q}$ lead to different mean-field approximations. For example, structured mean-field methods choose $\mathcal{Q}$ so that some dependencies between variables are retained. In this thesis, we use the fully factorized approximation, also called naive mean-field (NMF), because it leads to simple differentiable iterative updates.

In NMF, the approximate distribution factorizes over variables:
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_factorization}
    q(\XVEC)
    =
    \prod_{i\in\FGV} q_i(x_i).
\end{equation}
Although this approximation cannot represent all dependencies in the MRF, it gives tractable updates for the unary marginals $q_i$. Under the factorized approximation, the entropy decomposes as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_entropy}
    H(q)
    =
    -\sum_{i\in\FGV}\sum_{x_i\in\mathcal{X}_i}
    q_i(x_i)\log q_i(x_i),
\end{equation}
and the expected energy decomposes over factors:
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_expected_energy}
    \mathbb{E}_q[E(\XVEC)]
    =
    \sum_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}
    \sum_{x_{\FACTOR}\in\mathcal{X}_{\FACTOR}}
    E_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})
    \prod_{j\in N(\FACTOR)} q_j(x_j).
\end{equation}

The coordinate update for a variable $i$ is obtained by optimizing $q_i$ while keeping all other marginals fixed. The implementation used in this thesis follows the log-potential convention of Equation~\eqref{equ:log_potential_definition}, so the update is written in terms of factor scores $\GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}$ rather than energies $E_{\FACTOR}$.

For a factor $\FACTOR$ incident to variable $i$, define the mean-field score obtained by averaging the factor log-potential over the current marginals of the other variables:
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_factor_score}
    s_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x_i) = \sum_{x_{\FACTOR\setminus i}} \GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}(x_i,x_{\FACTOR\setminus i}) \prod_{j\in N(\FACTOR)\setminus\{i\}} q_j^{(t)}(x_j).
\end{equation}
The parallel NMF update is then obtained by summing these scores over the factors incident to $i$ and normalizing with a softmax:
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_softmax_update}
    \widetilde{q}_i^{(t+1)}(x_i) = \frac{
        \exp\left(
            \sum_{\FACTOR\in N(i)} s_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x_i)
        \right)
    }{
        \sum_{x'_i\in\mathcal{X}_i}
        \exp\left(
            \sum_{\FACTOR\in N(i)} s_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x'_i)
        \right)
    }.
\end{equation}

In practice, one can update variables sequentially, as in classical coordinate ascent, or update all variables in parallel using the marginals from the previous iteration. The latter form is particularly convenient for implementation on graphs and for unrolling a fixed number of inference iterations inside a differentiable model. A damped parallel update can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:nmf_damped_update}
    q_i^{(t+1)} = (1-\MFDAMPING)q_i^{(t)} + \MFDAMPING \,\widetilde{q}_i^{(t+1)},
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{q}_i^{(t+1)}$ is the normalized mean-field update and $\MFDAMPING\in(0,1]$ controls the size of the update toward the new marginal.

The NMF marginals are initialized uniformly, with $q_i^{(0)}(0)=q_i^{(0)}(1)=1/2$ for binary variables. This corresponds to an uninformative factorized distribution before the factor log-potentials are incorporated through the mean-field updates.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Naive Mean-Field Inference}
\label{alg:naive_mean_field}
\KwData{Factor graph $\FG=(\FGV\cup\FGF,\FGE)$, log-potentials $\{\GENEPARAM_{\FACTOR}\}_{\FACTOR\in\FGF}$, number of iterations $T$, update weight $\MFDAMPING$}
\KwResult{Approximate unary marginals $\{q_i\}_{i\in\FGV}$}
Initialize each $q_i^{(0)}$ uniformly over $\mathcal{X}_i$; for binary variables, set $q_i^{(0)}(0)=q_i^{(0)}(1)=1/2$\;
\For{$t=0,\dots,T-1$}{
    \ForEach{$(\FACTOR,i)\in\FGE$}{
        Compute the mean-field score $s_{\FACTOR\rightarrow i}^{(t)}(x_i)$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:nmf_factor_score}\;
    }
    \ForEach{$i\in\FGV$}{
        Compute $\widetilde{q}_i^{(t+1)}$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:nmf_softmax_update}\;
        Update $q_i^{(t+1)}$ using Equation~\eqref{equ:nmf_damped_update}\;
    }
}
\end{algorithm}

NMF is less expressive than LBP because it assumes independence under the approximate distribution and returns only unary marginals. In particular, it does not maintain explicit factor beliefs $\BELIEF_{\FACTOR}(x_{\FACTOR})$. However, its updates are simple, local, and differentiable. This makes fixed-iteration NMF useful as a lightweight structured inference layer, especially when comparing different ways of transforming learned MRF parameters into marginal information for decoding.


\subsubsection{Differentiable approximate inference}

Both LBP and NMF can be viewed as iterative algorithms composed of differentiable operations. Rather than necessarily running these methods until convergence, we often use a fixed number of iterations and treat the resulting computation as an inference layer inside a larger neural model. Gradients can then be backpropagated through the approximate inference procedure to train the parameters that define the MRF potentials. This view is closely related to the approach of \citet{domke_learning_2013}, who studies parameter learning when inference itself is approximate. In particular, Domke proposes learning through approximate marginal inference by differentiating through message-passing procedures and accounting for the errors introduced by approximate inference, including those arising from running only a fixed number of iterations.

This perspective is important for learning to solve BILPs. A neural network can predict local MRF parameters from an instance graph, approximate inference can transform those parameters into structured marginal information, and a decoder can use the resulting marginals to construct a candidate solution. The inference layer therefore acts as an inductive bias: it forces predictions to pass through the dependency structure encoded by the factor graph, rather than treating variables as independent labels. Although we do not incorporate Domke's correction-based learning scheme in the present work, it provides a natural direction for future extensions, where the training objective could explicitly compensate for the approximation errors induced by truncated LBP or NMF inference.

\subsubsection{Conditioning and clamping}
\label{subsec:background_conditional_clamping}

Inference is often performed not only on the original MRF, but also on a conditional distribution where some variables are fixed. Given a set of fixed variables $X_A=x_A$, conditional inference asks for marginals or MAP assignments over the remaining variables $X_{V\setminus A}$ under
\begin{equation}
p(x_{V\setminus A}\mid x_A)
=
\frac{p(x_{V\setminus A},x_A)}
{\sum_{x'_{V\setminus A}}p(x'_{V\setminus A},x_A)}.
\end{equation}
In a factor graph, this conditioning operation can be implemented by \emph{clamping} the variables in $A$ to their fixed values. Factors incident to clamped variables are then restricted to local configurations consistent with those values, producing a reduced inference problem over the unclamped variables.

This operation is useful whenever partial information is available, when one wants to study the effect of fixing part of an assignment, or when inference is performed sequentially on a progressively restricted model. The specific use of conditioning in the decoding procedures studied in this thesis is introduced later when those methods are defined.

\subsection{Chapter synthesis and transition}

This chapter introduced the three foundations used throughout the remainder of the thesis. Integer linear programming provides the formal language for the discrete optimization problems considered here: binary decision variables define candidate assignments, constraints define feasibility, and objective functions define solution quality. Machine learning provides the tools for learning from families of such instances, with graph neural networks and attention-based models allowing predictions to depend on the structure of the variable-constraint graph.

Markov Random Fields then provide the structured probabilistic framework that connects these ingredients. By representing dependencies among BILP solution variables through factors and log-potentials, MRFs make it possible to transform learned local information into structured marginal beliefs using approximate inference methods such as LBP and NMF. This perspective is central to the thesis: learned predictions are not treated as independent variable-wise decisions, but are passed through graphical-model structure that reflects the constraints of the underlying optimization problem.

The canonical feasibility-cost parameterization of Section~\ref{subsec:canonical-feasibility-cost-param} becomes the CMRF baseline in Chapter~\ref{chapter:3_differentiable_mrf_inference_for_BILPs}, while the differentiable LBP and NMF procedures of Sections~\ref{subsec:background_lbp} and~\ref{subsec:background_mean_field} are used as inference layers in Sections~\ref{subsec:chapter_3_differentiable_inference} and~\ref{sec:state_conditioned_mrf_refinement}.

The same framework also exposes two limitations addressed in later chapters. First, exact factor tables scale exponentially with factor arity, which becomes problematic for high-arity constraints such as those appearing in Set Cover. Second, repeated inference under changing conditions can be computationally expensive. These limitations motivate the approximate factor-graph parameterizations and learned inference-based methods developed in the following chapters.
