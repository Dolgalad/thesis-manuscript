MRF distribution
\[ p_{\theta}(x \mid P) = \frac{1}{Z(\theta)} \exp\!\left( \left\langle \theta(P;\omega), \phi(x) \right\rangle \right) \]
Exact likelihood objective
\[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\omega) &= -\log p_{\theta(P;\omega)}(y \mid P) \\ &= \log Z(\theta) - \left\langle \theta(P;\omega), \phi(y) \right\rangle \end{aligned} \]
Computing \(Z(\theta)\) requires summing over all assignments: \[ Z(\theta)=\sum_{x'\in\mathcal{X}} \exp\!\left(\left\langle\theta,\phi(x')\right\rangle\right) \] Intractable for loopy, high-dimensional MRFs.
Approximation strategy
Partition function approximate \(\log Z(\theta)\) with the Bethe free energy.
Variable marginals train \(\mu_v(y_v)\) as binary classification targets.
Factor marginals train \(\mu_\alpha(y_\alpha)\) as multi-class classification over local configurations.
Bethe/BP and surrogate learning .
This slide simplifies the transition from standard neural network training to fitting an MRF. The natural objective is the negative log-likelihood of the reference assignment under the MRF distribution. This objective contains the log-partition function, which requires summing over all possible binary assignments and is therefore intractable for the loopy high-dimensional factor graphs used in the thesis. The practical alternative is to replace exact likelihood quantities by approximate inference quantities: the Bethe free energy gives an approximation to the partition function, while variable and factor marginals give local supervised classification targets.
