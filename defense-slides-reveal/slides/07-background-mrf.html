Introduction to Markov Random Fields and factor graphs .

Random variables
\[ X = (X_i)_{1 \leq i \leq n}, \qquad X_i \in \{0,1\} \]
Factor graph
\[ G_F = (\mathcal{V} \cup \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{E}_F) \]
variables \(i \in \mathcal{V}\), factors \(\alpha \in \mathcal{F}\)
\[ p(x) = \frac{1}{Z} \prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{F}} \psi_{\alpha}\!\left(x_{N(\alpha)}\right) \]
\[ Z = \sum_x \prod_{\alpha \in \mathcal{F}} \psi_{\alpha}\!\left(x_{N(\alpha)}\right) \]
Example factorization
Product of local potentials
\[ \begin{aligned} p(x_1,x_2,x_3) &= \frac{1}{Z} \psi_{\alpha_1}(x_1,x_2) \\ &\quad \psi_{\alpha_2}(x_1,x_2,x_3) \\ &\quad \psi_{\alpha_3}(x_2,x_3) \end{aligned} \]
This slide introduces Markov random fields through factor graphs. Avoid discussing exponential-family parametrizations here. The key point is the factorization: the joint distribution is a normalized product of local potentials, each attached to a factor node and depending only on its neighboring variables.
